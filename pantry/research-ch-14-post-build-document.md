## 3. Application Domain Examples

(Domain coverage map: Ch 14 sits in simulation + book retrospective.)

Worked example post-build document for sorting-algorithm simulation, five sections:

1. **What I built (plain):** "Interactive bubble-sort visualization showing why it's O(n²). Students step through comparisons; simulation highlights compared/moved/sorted regions. Deployed via Codex Artifacts; embedded in class website."
2. **What I delegated to Codex and why:** "HTML scaffold and CSS layout — pattern work. Bubble-sort logic — well-specified with clear test cases."
3. **What I kept for myself and why:** "Intent Layer; DESIGN.md color and interaction decisions; the decision to teach bubble sort *as counterexample to efficiency*, not as a tool."
4. **What I learned that I didn't know before:** "Codex iterates on visual output if given screenshot to compare. DESIGN.md six-color constraint was most useful thing I did — forced me to decide what the simulation was *for*."
5. **What I would do differently:** "Start with Intent Layer earlier; drafted it after AGENTS.md and DESIGN.md, and it changed what would have gone in those. Reverse order next time. Step controls (Step 4) should have been specified before sort logic (Step 3) — I rebuilt Step 3."

The chapter walks through with teacher voice. Honesty is the discipline.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

### Prior knowledge required
Teacher has finished book.

### Common misconceptions
- **"Post-build is bureaucratic."** Five sections; <30 minutes.
- **"I'll write it when I have time."** Now.
- **"My document should be modest."** Performative humility is failure.

### Effective sequences
- **Walk through five sections.**
- **Show Seth's or author's post-build.**
- **Reader writes their own** (apply-level exercise).

### Known failure modes
- **Ceremony chapter.** Portable artifact.
- **Over-claiming "you're done."**
- **Book's voice continuing too long.**

### What separates understanding from memorization
A reader who *understands* Ch 14 has produced post-build document with specific "what I would do differently" naming a real decision they would reverse. A reader who memorized Ch 14 produces generic document.
